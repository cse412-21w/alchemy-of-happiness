Introduction to Happiness



Our data set comes from World Happiness Report. It is a survey of the global happiness and gain recognitions from governments, organizations and civil societies.

The reports review the state of happiness in the world today and show how the new science of happiness explains personal and national variations in happiness.

To achieve our goal, we selected 4 potential factors in the report, trust in government, economy, life expectancy, and GDP per capita. We are hoping to see how does each factor effect the happiness score? Which factor is the leading cause to a country's happiness.

Health = Happiness



The scatter plot illustrates the relationship between the two variables, showing all five years of data with X-axis being Happiness Score and Y-axis being Life Expectancy.

We divided the points into four sub-sections by high/low life expectancy & high/low happiness score. As we can see that majority of the data is divided into Quadrant I (+,+) and Quadrant III (-,-), which means life expectancy directly relates to its happiness score.

More specificly, high life expectancy countries has high happiness score and, conversely, low life expectancy countries has low happiness score.

Money = Happiness?



On average, a country with high GDP per capita will have a high happiness score.

However, does it really implies that GDP per capita is the most important factor of the happiness level of a country?

How much impact does GDP have on the happiness level?

Case Study



In the default setting, data of 5 countries, Armenia, Australia, Bangladesh, Bhutan, and Bolivia, are presented. A clear positive trend is shown when looking at Bangladesh, Bolivia, and Australia. For the three countries, an increase in GDP corresponds with an increase in the happiness score.

However, if we look at Armenia, Bhutan, and Bolivia, they all have similar GDP but different happiness scores. It shows, with the same GDP, a country could improve its happiness level in other ways.

Conclusion



We studied four potential factors of happiness scores: trust in government, economy, Life expectancy, and GDP per capita. Over the four factors, trust in government and economy has less effect on a countries happiness score. GDP per capita shows a positive correlation with happiness score but the variance stays high. Life expectancy is the top one influential factor to happiness. Countries with a high average people's life expectancy always have a high happiness score.

To sum up, the rank of the four affective factors of happiness is Life expectancy, and GDP per capita, trust in government, and economy (from most influential to no influence). Also, trust and economy tend to have less effect on happiness score. Therefore, a country with a high life expectancy and high GDP per capita tends to be a happy place.
